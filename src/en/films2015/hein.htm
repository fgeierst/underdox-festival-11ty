The Long Night of UNDERGROUND: Wilhelm Hein


wilhelm hein is: european avantgarde. alternative cinema. experimental film. material aethetics. UNDERGROUND.

You killed the Undergroundfilm or The Real Meaning of Kunst bleibt ... bleibt ...

Wilhelm Hein
Deutschland 1989-2013
720 min – 16mm – ohne Dialoge – separater Ton auf CD
performed on three 16mm-projectors – length: ca. 360 min

"There are different tonalities at play: the poignant and the ironic, the banal and the extraordinary, the ridiculous, the irreverent, the abject, the sublime… Given the unquantifiable amount of sequences, YOU KILLED THE UNDERGROUND FILM… is impossible to summarize. To start with, the film follows ist own inherent logic, with no apparent beginning nor end; it has no narrative, at least no story – but „big stories“, as Hein says, gradually become apparent." – Cécil Chich

saturday 10 oct 10.30 pm galerie der künstler

"Shot over two decades, YOU KILLED THE UNDERGROUND FILM… is still, Hein says, 'a work in progress'. The shooting goes on with every wandering, encounter, or personal occasion; the editing is contantly remade; and the separate accompanying soundtrack changes at every projection. Nothing is ever really fixed, so each viewing is a unique variation of the film, and each screening a rare performance. Formally, the film is at once a personal notebook, a traveller’s records, a collage of ready-mades, a visual essay on history, an album of admired inspirers and friends, a miditation on suffering, a love poem…

Hein is evidently of this rare breed of experimental filmmakers who face the world alone with their hand held 16mm camera, constantly questioning what he sees and what it means to make a film – with reality and perception existing in an interrelated discourse, just like the anode and the cathode of an electric current. The camera is subjective, using a variety of techniques, and the film also includes sequences of found footage…

Themes start to emerge, and I sense undercurrents at work within the film, lines of enquiries, obsessions even. Coming and crashing, fusing or clashing, they are played over and over again, but never in the same way. In the two hours that I know, I descern three main undercurrents: one on art and film, clearly proclaimed; one, more grave and sombre, on the tormendet history of Germany and Eastern Europe; and a third one, somewhat more secretive, about sex, or more precisely the body. And as the film relfects upon ist own fascinating capturing power, meanings of the three undercurrents are weaved together, create echoes, and reverberate upon each other." – Cécile Chich
